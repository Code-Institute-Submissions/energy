2.Sources of the Future

Solar

Solar energy uses photovoltaic (PV) panels to capture energy from photons released by the sun. These PV panels have solar cells most commonly made of positive and negative layers silicon with have conductors attached to them. When the photons hit the cells they absorb some of the energy which knocks free electrons and forms a circuit which generates electricity.

Key Solar Energy Facts

Excepting pollution generated during the PV panel manufacturing process, solar power produces no pollution.

The cost of solar panels has dropped 80% since 2008 and is expected to keep falling.

There are over 57 million sq. miles of land on earth and in order to power the entire earth on solar energy we would need to install solar panels on over 191,00 sq miles.

Current Barriers to Large Scale Implementation

  • Although the price is steadily falling the start-up costs are still relatively high and the time it takes for return on investment is long.
  • Large battery banks are needed with current battery technology
  • Current grid technology is not set up to be integrated with solar systems.
  • Energy generated is weather dependent.

Wind

How it Works

Wind energy is generated by wind turbines. The blades of a wind turbine rotate when hit by the wind due to a transfer of kinetic energy from the wind to the blades of the turbine. The lnger the blades and the stronger the wind the more energy will be generated. This kinetic energy is converted into electrical energy by a generator and is then transformed to an appropriate voltage for usage.

Key Wind Energy Facts

Like solar energy, wind energy produces no pollution during energy generation.

A single large wind turbine is enough to power 600 average homes.

Wind energy prices have dropped 66% since 2009.

Current Barriers to Large Scale Implementation

  • Set up costs,while they are falling are still quite high
  • Large battery banks are needed with current battery technology
  • Efficiency of wind turbines is still only around 60%
  • Energy generated is weather dependent.

Renewables Success Story:Germany

In 2000, the German government passed the Renewable Energy Law or Energiewende.When they passed the law, Germany was getting 7% of its electricity from renewable sources. Today, with an increase in overall energy generated, Germany gets ~34% of its electricity from renewable energy.They have the world's largest wind power sector and the worlds second largest solar power market. The success in Germany shows the importance of good policy as the law allowed for guaranteed access to the grid for renewable energy producers and introduced tarrifs which helped to subsidize the market.

A Brief History Of Battery Technology

Click through the years to read the major milestones in battery development.

In 1780, while cutting a frog leg Luigi Aloisio Galvani, an Italian scientist discovered that an electric spark caused the leg to twitch.He thought he was observing a kind of "animal electricity" but it was later discovered by Alessandro Volta that this was the result of the interaction between his steel scalpel and a brass hook.
Alessandro Volta, an Italian physicist creates the first true battery. This battery was made of stacks of discs of copper and zinc separated by cloth soaked in brine. Water connected each end to produce a stable current.
Raymond Gaston Planté invents the lead acid battery. This is the first rechargable battery and a version of it is still in production today.
Georges Leclanché creates the Leclanché cell a precursor to the modern dry cell. The cell was composed of an electrolyte in the form of ammonium chloride a carbon cathode, zinc anode and magnesium dioxide.
In 1899 Ernst Waldman Junger invented the nickel cadmium battery. This is a rechargable battery that uses nickel oxide hydroxide and metallic cadmium as electrodes.
Edison invents eletric battery
Lithium ion batteries developed

Current Areas of Research

Currently, there is no clear successor to the lithium ion battery and although there have been some small incremental improvements in lithium ion performance in recent years the general consensus is that it is tending towards the limit of what it can do.There are however there are several promising areas of research although they face a number of barriers. One of these is the fact that when it comes to battery development it often seems like improving performance in one metric can lead to a decrease in performance in another.Additionally, as there are many competing areas of research and a limited amount of funding compared to the high cost of development several projects have difficulty getting off the ground in any meaningful way.There are however there areas of development that appear promising in terms of delivery results in the future.

  1. Hydrogen Fuel Cells
    Hydrogen can be produced with wind and solar energy and used to power fuel cells. Fuel cells are a little different from a traditional battery in that as long as there is a constant source of fuel and oxygen they do not need to recharge.
  2. Lithium-Sulphur
    A lithium sulphur battery could have a capacity of three to five times higher than that of a lithium ion battery. Development is currently trying to combat thehigh risk of electrode breakdown in these types of batteries.
  3. Redox Flow
    A promising solution for energy storage at the grid scale.Right now the problems it is trying to overcomefor a more widespread implementation are operating temperature,high cost and low energy density.

Smart Grid

The grid technology used by much of the world today is fundamentally the same as when it was developed.As newer sources of energy are developedand energy demand increases, upgrading grid tech can facilitate uninterupted clean energy and put greater power into the hands of the consumer.So what are the elements of the smart grid?

Smart power meters will allow for two way communication between big companies,consumers and small-scale combination consumer producers.It will give customers access to all their power data allowing them to have a dynamic relationship with the grid and make informed energy decisions.
Smart distribution will allow for widespread distributed generation through increased automation,smart sensors and richer data streams and communication. It wil also feature better superconducting cables for improved transmission.
Smart generation uses artificial intelligence and other means to model power generation for optimized efficient performance. It incorporates and processes feedback from multiple points on the grid.
Smart substations are crucial for monitoring operational data adn overall grid performance and allows for any issues to be addressed quickly.

4.Energy Efficiency

What is Energy Efficiency

Energy efficiency means using less energy for a particular task. The idea is that lightbulbs, appliances etc can all perform the same tasks,just as well, using less power. This adds savings to the consumer,helps reduce the use of fossil fuels and makes renewable energy sources go further.So how can energy efficiency be effected?It all comes down to forming good policy on building design and management and appliance manufacture.

Policy:The Driver Towards Change

Ultimately, for widespread adoption of energy efficient pratices policy will be needed. The International Energy Agency recommends the following 25 policy perscriptions.

  1. Energy efficiency data collection and indicators
  2. Strategies and action plans
  3. Competitive energy markets, with appropriate regulation
  4. Private investment in energy efficiency
  5. Monitoring, enforcement and evaluation of policies and measures
  6. Mandatory building energy codes and minimum energy performance
  7. Aiming for net-zero energy consumption in buildings
  8. Improving the energy efficiency of existing buildings
  9. Building energy labels or certificates
  10. Improved energy performance of building components and systems
  11. Mandatory MEPS and labels for appliances and equipment
  12. Test standards and measurement protocols for appliances and equipment
  13. Market transformation policies for appliances and equipment
  14. Phase-out of inefficient lighting products and systems
  15. Energy-efficient lighting systems
  16. Mandatory vehicle fuel efficiency standards
  17. Measures to improve vehicle fuel efficiency
  18. Fuel-efficient non-engine components
  19. Improving vehicle operational efficiency through eco-driving and other measures
  20. Transport system efficiency
  21. Energy management in industry
  22. High-efficiency industrial equipment and systems
  23. Energy efficiency services for small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs)
  24. Complementary policies to support industrial energy efficiency
  25. Energy utilities and end-use energy efficiency
